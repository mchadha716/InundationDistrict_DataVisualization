RISING SEA LEVELS

Ocean levels are rising at unrpecedented rates. Due to byproducts of climate change like greenhouse gases, melting glaciers, and thermal expansion, experts worry that rising sea levels will "wreak havoc on coastal cities across the world."

As a coastal city, Boston experiences the consequences of changes in sea level. Though the city already experiences regular flooding, Boston is predicted to face up to 1.5 feet of sea-level rise by 2050.

Situated on the water, Boston's Seaport district is at risk of serious damage to buildings and roads as a result of future flooding. The following data visualizations outline how changes in sea level will impact flooding in Seaport.

Changes in sea level can affect many aspects of a city's ecosystem. Two common side effects of sea-level rise that may impact the Seaport neighborhood are increased flooding and heavy precipitation.

Below are visualizations that outline the past, present and future of these climate patterns in Boston.

Which trend would you like to explore?

Since the 1950s, heavy precipitation has become more common around the world. As the planet continues to warm, this trend will continue, because warmer air can hold more water vapor. In the United States, the Midwest and Northeast have experienced the largest increase in frequency of heavy precipitation events.

The most immediate threat of heavy precipitation is flooding. In urban areas such as Boston, floods have the ability to devastate roads and infrastructure, harm human health, and overwhelm sewer systems.

1966-1996

Extreme weather events such as prolonged droughts and flash flooding have been the primary indicators of changes to precipitation rates in recent years. From the mid-1960s to the mid-1980s, the average inches of precipitation per year in the Northeast remained steady.

This number rose in the years between 1987 and 1996, likely due to extreme weather events at the time.

1997-2016

From 1997 to 2006, the average inches of precipitation per year increased even more dramatically than in the previous decade; then plateaued from 2007 to 2016. The result has been “heavy rain and snow resulting in about 1 to 2 inches of water a day depending on location since 1996.”

A study published in Weather and Climate Extremes explained that these instances of extreme precipitation result from an increase of greenhouse gasses caused by human activity and variable surface temperatures in the Atlantic Ocean.

Floods are among the most common natural disasters in the United States. They can cause loss of human life, destruction of property and an increase in waterborne diseases. As global warming continues to exacerbate rises in sea level, it “detectably influences” many variables that cause flooding, according to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change.

Projections show that cities along coastlines could experience over 10 feet of sea-level rise within the next 100 years. Boston has historically surpassed national averages in annual flooding. Studies show this trend is likely to continue.

1920-1990

Early data shows Boston's location on the water may have boosted its susptability to flooding.

From 1920 to 1990, the number of high-tide flood days, days on which tides inundated low-lying areas, increased steadily across the nation. But Boston consistently recorded more high-tide flood days per decade than the national average.

1990-2010

In just two decades between 1990 and 2010, the number of high-tide flood days skyrocketed throughout the United States. But again, Boston faced an especially drastic spike in high-tide flooding. The number of flood days in Boston tripled from 1990 to 2010, while the national average doubled.

Much of Boston’s infrastructure will not be prepared to face continuous increases in annual flood days. Dated drainage systems that are common in one the United States' oldest cities are not equipped to handle repeated floods.

2000-2050

By 2050, Boston is predicted to experience 70 high-tide flood days per year, which would amount to more than four times as many high-tide flood days per decade compared to 2010.

Such a drastic rise in flooding “endangers crucial infrastructure in Boston, including waste and stormwater systems, MBTA lines, and roads.”

Flooding and heavy precipitation events caused by rising sea levels have plagued the Boston area. Predictions show these extreme weather events will only worsen in years to come.

What do you think the future will look like?

The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, NOAA, has found that even by intermediate estimates, sea levels in Boston will rise by over a foot by 2050. High estimates are more severe and predict 21.3 inches of sea-level rise by 2050. These projections only become more drastic with time. Both of these estimates are far above the city code for development, which defines what degree of potential flood risk is safe for infrastructure development.

Boston endures worse high-tide flooding than most cities in the United States. By NOAA's estimates, large portions of Boston, including Seaport, will be swallowed up by the sea in the coming century.

We hope that this project will increase the Boston community's awareness of the problem, as well as motivate further research on the effect of rising sea levels in Boston.

Project G01: Rising Sea Levels, DS 4200 S22

Mira Chadha, Michelle Lo, Annetta Stogniew

Service-Learning Course Project as part of Ds 4200 S22 Information Presentation & Visualization, taught by Prof. Cody Dunne, Data Visualization @ Khoury, Northeastern University.

Abstract

This project will consist of data visualizations about rising sea levels. The graphics will show how flooding is increasing, how sea levels are rising, and how vulnerable various buildings will be to sea levels over time. This project is beneficial to understanding more about climate change and how it can affect coastal cities such as Boston. The resulting data will be used in an upcoming documentary film about how rising sea levels will affect Boston, particularly its Seaport neighborhood.

Rising sea levels are being caused by rapidly melting ice caps and rising global average temperatures. As climate change stands now, these sea levels will “wreak havoc on coastal cities across the world.” Some cities listed by a United Nations report include Mumbai, New York, Shanghai, and Miami. Boston, a city that already experiences regular flooding, is predicted to face up to 1.5 feet of sea-level rise by 2050. This would primarily affect buildings in the Seaport district. Data visualizations that show how flooding is increasing, how sea levels are rising, and how vulnerable buildings will be to rising sea levels will increase the Boston community’s awareness of the problem as well as motivate further research on the effect of rising sea levels in Boston. These data visualizations are planned to appear in an upcoming documentary film.
