Project G01: Rising Sea Levels, DS 4200 S22

Mira Chadha, Michelle Lo, Annetta Stogniew

Service-Learning Course Project as part of Ds 4200 S22 Information Presentation & Visualization, taught by Prof. Cody Dunne, Data Visualization @ Khoury, Northeastern University.

Abstract

This project will consist of data visualizations about rising sea levels. The graphics will show how flooding is increasing, how sea levels are rising, and how vulnerable various buildings will be to sea levels over time. This project is beneficial to understanding more about climate change and how it can affect coastal cities such as Boston. The resulting data will be used in an upcoming documentary film about how rising sea levels will affect Boston, particularly its Seaport neighborhood.

Rising sea levels are being caused by rapidly melting ice caps and rising global average temperatures. As climate change stands now, these sea levels will “wreak havoc on coastal cities across the world.” Some cities listed by a United Nations report include Mumbai, New York, Shanghai, and Miami. Boston, a city that already experiences regular flooding, is predicted to face up to 1.5 feet of sea-level rise by 2050. This would primarily affect buildings in the Seaport district. Data visualizations that show how flooding is increasing, how sea levels are rising, and how vulnerable buildings will be to rising sea levels will increase the Boston community’s awareness of the problem as well as motivate further research on the effect of rising sea levels in Boston. These data visualizations are planned to appear in an upcoming documentary film.

Storyboard

Our storyboard follows.

RISING SEA LEVELS

The oceans are rising: greenhouse gases, melting glaciers, and thermal expansion have and will continue to devastate infrastructure and coastal habitats. As climate change stands now, these sea levels will "wreak havoc on coastal cities across the world." One city in particular that will face these changes is Boston. Though it already experiences regular flooding, it is predicted to face up to 1.5 feet of sea-level rise by 2050. Such a rise in sea levels will primarily affect buildings in the Seaport district. The data visualizations below display how flooding is increasing, how sea levels are rising, and how vulnerable buildings are to such concerns. We hope that this project will increases the Boston community's awareness of the problem, as well as motivate further research on the effect of rising sea levels in Boston.

Which trend would you like to explore?

Flooding

Floods are among the most common natural disasters in the United States. They devastate lives, natural habitats and city infrastructure alike, and they are on a continuously upward trend. Global warming continues to exacerbate rises in sea level, and has been noted by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change to “detectably influence” many variables that play a part in flooding.

Projections currently show that cities along coastlines could experience over ten feet of sea level rise within a century. The vast loss of lives and property damage that such flooding has the potential to cause is indisputable. Indeed, Boston’s Seaport district is particularly vulnerable to such concerns.

1920-1990

The seventy years between 1920 and 1990 showed a steady increase in the national average number of high-tide flooding days. Boston in particular experienced a larger increase in such flood days, with its peak occurring during the 1970s, at 35 days compared to the national average of 8.4 days. Such an increase was certainly an early indication of the drastically negative effects that climate change poses to a coastal city.
